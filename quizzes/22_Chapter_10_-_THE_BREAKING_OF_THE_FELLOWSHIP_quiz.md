# Reading Comprehension Quiz: 22_Chapter_10_-_THE_BREAKING_OF_THE_FELLOWSHIP.md

**Difficulty:** Advanced | **Questions:** 10 | **Time suggested:** 30–40 minutes

**Instructions:** Each question below is followed by a relevant passage from the text. Choose the single best answer (A–E) for each question. Focus on what the text *implies* rather than what it explicitly states. Consider tone, subtext, character psychology, and authorial choices in your reasoning.

---

### Question 1 `[CIM/ANT]` `[Advanced]`

Aragorn’s statement to Frodo that "In this matter I cannot advise you" primarily reflects:

> **Relevant Passage:**
> "'Well, Frodo,' said Aragorn at last. 'I fear that the burden is laid upon you. ... In this matter I cannot advise you. I am not Gandalf... Such is your fate.'"

- **A.** Aragorn’s lack of confidence in his own decision-making abilities.
- **B.** A profound respect for the sanctity of the individual "will" and the recognition that the ultimate choice of the Ring-bearer must be a free act of conscience, uncoerced by even the highest authority.
- **C.** His desire to blame Frodo if the mission eventually fails.
- **D.** His belief that Gandalf would have already made the choice for them.
- **E.** A sign that he is secretly hoping Frodo will choose Minas Tirith.

### Question 2 `[CIM/PIS]` `[Advanced]`

Boromir’s initial approach to Frodo in the woods, claiming he was "afraid for you" and seeking "wisdom" in a private talk, is characterized by:

> **Relevant Passage:**
> "'I was afraid for you, Frodo,' he said, coming forward. ... 'Where there are so many, all speech becomes a debate without end. But two together may perhaps find wisdom.'"

- **A.** A genuine and selfless concern for Frodo’s physical safety.
- **B.** A deceptive use of the language of friendship and "wisdom" to create an isolated psychological space where he can more easily exert pressure on Frodo to follow his own agenda.
- **C.** Boromir’s belief that he is more wise than Aragorn.
- **D.** His desire to warn Frodo about the Orcs on Amon Lhaw.
- **E.** A sign that Boromir has already forgotten about the Ring.

### Question 3 `[CIM/ANT]` `[Advanced]`

Frodo’s admission that he is "simply afraid" but that his "mind is clearer now" after hearing Boromir’s full argument indicates:

> **Relevant Passage:**
> "'No, I am afraid,' said Frodo. 'Simply afraid. But I am glad to have heard you speak so fully. My mind is clearer now.'"

- **A.** Frodo’s decision to follow Boromir to Minas Tirith.
- **B.** His realization that the "wisdom" Boromir offers is actually the very corruption he must avoid, thereby clarifying his own need to separate himself from the Company to protect the quest.
- **C.** His belief that Boromir’s plan is the most logical survival strategy.
- **D.** A sign that Frodo is no longer afraid of the Black Riders.
- **E.** His gratitude for Boromir’s military expertise.

### Question 4 `[PIS/SCA]` `[Advanced]`

Boromir’s outburst ("If any mortals have claim to the Ring, it is the men of Numenor, and not Halflings") reveals a worldview based on:

> **Relevant Passage:**
> "'...If any mortals have claim to the Ring, it is the men of Numenor, and not Halflings. It is not yours save by unhappy chance. It might have been mine. It should be mine. Give it to me!'"

- **A.** A commitment to the democratic ideals of the Free Peoples.
- **B.** An ethno-nationalist sense of entitlement and a hierarchy of "merit" based on ancestral power, which blinds him to the moral danger of the Ring itself.
- **C.** His belief that Bilbo stole the Ring from a Man of Gondor.
- **D.** A tactical plan to use the Ring to rebuild the city of Osgiliath.
- **E.** His respect for the "unhappy chance" that brought the Ring to the Shire.

### Question 5 `[ACN/ANT]` `[Advanced]`

Frodo’s vision on the Seat of Seeing, where the world seems to have "shrunk" and he sees "signs of war" everywhere, symbolizes:

> **Relevant Passage:**
> "The world seemed to have shrunk and fallen silent. ... everywhere he looked he saw the signs of war. ... All the power of the Dark Lord was in motion."

- **A.** The physical limitations of the ancient Numenorean technology.
- **B.** The psychological and spiritual "encroachment" of the War of the Ring, where the vastness of the world is being replaced by a singular, suffocating reality of conflict and shadow.
- **C.** A sign that Frodo is becoming a powerful wizard like Saruman.
- **D.** The beauty of Middle-earth’s geography.
- **E.** A map of the places Frodo needs to visit next.

### Question 6 `[CIM/SCA]` `[Advanced]`

Frodo’s struggle between the "Voice" and the "Eye" on the hilltop represents a confrontation between:

> **Relevant Passage:**
> "The two powers strove in him. For a moment, perfectly balanced between their piercing points, he writhed, tormented. Suddenly he was aware of himself again, Frodo, neither the Voice nor the Eye: free to choose..."

- **A.** Gandalf and Saruman fighting for control of his mind.
- **B.** Two external, competing authoritarian wills (Sauron’s desire to possess and Gandalf’s desire to protect), which temporarily negate Frodo’s own agency until he reclaims his "self" through a moment of free choice.
- **C.** His own conscience and his greed for the Ring.
- **D.** A visual hallucination caused by the sun.
- **E.** His desire to stay in Rivendell vs. his desire to go home.

### Question 7 `[CIM/ANT]` `[Advanced]`

Frodo’s final decision to "go alone" is motivated by:

> **Relevant Passage:**
> "'...the evil of the Ring is already at work even in the Company, and the Ring must leave them before it does more harm. I will go alone. Some I cannot trust, and those I can trust are too dear to me...'"

- **A.** A selfish desire to have all the "glory" of the quest for himself.
- **B.** A combination of strategic necessity (avoiding the Ring's corrosive influence on his friends) and a deeply sacrificial love that refuses to lead those he cares for into certain death.
- **C.** His anger at Sam for being too talkative.
- **D.** A belief that he is the only one strong enough to face Sauron.
- **E.** A sign that he has lost all faith in the Company after Boromir’s attack.

### Question 8 `[CIM/PIS]` `[Advanced]`

Sam’s realization ("Your legs are too short, so use your head!") and his subsequent sprint to the boats illustrate:

> **Relevant Passage:**
> "'Whoa, Sam Gamgee!' he said aloud. 'Your legs are too short, so use your head! ... Boromir isn't lying... but he hasn't told us everything. ... He's got to get back to the boats. Back to the boats, Sam, like lightning!'"

- **A.** Sam’s belief that he is more intelligent than Aragorn.
- **B.** His intimate, intuitive understanding of Frodo’s thought processes and "Baggins" character, which allows him to bypass the confusion of the taller folk and predict Frodo's actual movements.
- **C.** His fear of being left alone with the "Big People."
- **D.** A sign that Sam is becoming tired of walking.
- **E.** His desire to steal one of the boats for himself.

### Question 9 `[ANT/PIS]` `[Advanced]`

Frodo’s reaction to Sam’s near-drowning—calling him "the worst of all the confounded nuisances"—carries a subtext of:

> **Relevant Passage:**
> "'Of all the confounded nuisances you are the worst, Sam!' he said. ... Frodo actually laughed. A sudden warmth and gladness touched his heart. ... 'It is no good trying to escape you.'"

- **A.** Genuine annoyance and frustration at Sam’s incompetence.
- **B.** A deeply affectionate, albeit gruff, recognition of Sam’s unbreakable loyalty, which provides a moment of healing "warmth" that breaks Frodo's resolve to be a solitary martyr.
- **C.** His belief that Sam is trying to sabotage the mission.
- **D.** A sign that Frodo is losing his temper due to the Ring’s influence.
- **E.** His hope that Sam will go back to the others.

### Question 10 `[ACN/SCA]` `[Advanced]`

The final image of Frodo and Sam "seeking a path... down into the Land of Shadow" marks a narrative transition to:

- **A.** The end of the war against Sauron.
- **B.** The ultimate "breaking" of the Fellowship’s safety and the beginning of the most desperate, isolated, and spiritually taxing stage of the journey, where the two "smallest" characters face the "greatest" evil alone.

- **D.** A return to the Shire.
