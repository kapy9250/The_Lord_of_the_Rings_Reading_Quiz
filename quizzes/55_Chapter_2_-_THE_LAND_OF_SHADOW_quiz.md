# Reading Comprehension Quiz: 55_Chapter_2_-_THE_LAND_OF_SHADOW.md

**Difficulty:** Advanced | **Questions:** 10 | **Time suggested:** 30–40 minutes

**Instructions:** Each question below is followed by a relevant passage from the text. Choose the single best answer (A–E) for each question. Focus on what the text *implies* rather than what it explicitly states. Consider tone, subtext, character psychology, and authorial choices in your reasoning.

---

### Question 1 `[CIM/ANT]` `[Advanced]`

Frodo’s decision to throw away his orc-mail, saying "The Ring is enough. This extra weight is killing me," reveals:

> **Relevant Passage:**
> "'It's no good, Sam. ... Even my mithril-coat seemed heavy when I was tired. This is far heavier. ... The Ring is enough. This extra weight is killing me. It must go.'"

- **A.** His strategic plan to move faster.
- **B.** A state of extreme, internal "narrowing," where the spiritual and physical weight of the Ring has become the absolute center of his existence, making all other forms of protection seem like intolerable distractions.
- **C.** Evidence that the orc-mail is actually cursed.
- **D.** His belief that he will never have to fight again.
- **E.** A sign that Frodo is starting to trust the Orcs to protect him.

### Question 2 `[ACN/ANT]` `[Advanced]`

The description of the "thorny bushes" in Mordor—with thorns "a foot long" that are "tough as wire and as clinging as claws"—characterizes the land’s nature as:

> **Relevant Passage:**
> "The thorns and briars were as tough as wire and as clinging as claws. ... things still grew, harsh, twisted, bitter, struggling for life."

- **A.** A successful biological defense against invaders.
- **B.** A perversion of nature, where the regenerative power of growth has been "twisted" into a malevolent, aggressive, and agonizing architecture of pain that mirrors the Shadow's own malice.
- **C.** Evidence that the Orcs are very good at botanical engineering.
- **D.** A sign that the hobbits are losing their ability to perceive beauty.
- **E.** A humorous comparison to the blackberry bushes of the Shire.

### Question 3 `[CIM/ANT]` `[Advanced]`

Frodo’s feeling that the "blind dark seems to be getting into my heart" and his inability to see the Brandywine in his mind reveal:

> **Relevant Passage:**
> "'I feel much lighter. I can go on now. But this blind dark seems to be getting into my heart. ... I tried to remember the Brandywine... But I can't see them now.'"

- **A.** A literal loss of his visual memory.
- **B.** The progressive spiritual "erasure" caused by the Ring and the Shadow, where the internal sanctuary of his memories is being overwritten by the encroaching nothingness of Mordor.
- **C.** Evidence that Frodo is catching a cold from the damp air.
- **D.** A sign that he is becoming as "changed" as Gollum.
- **E.** His desire to find a new home in the Land of Shadow.

### Question 4 `[PIS/SCA]` `[Advanced]`

The "cry of woe and dismay" from the Nazgûl flying high above signifies to the reader (though not fully to the hobbits) that:

> **Relevant Passage:**

- **B.** The turning tide of the larger war (specifically the death of the Witch-king), providing a moment of "unseen" dramatic irony where the hobbits' isolation is punctured by a global shift in the balance of power.
- **C.** A sign that Sauron has given up on finding the Ring.
- **D.** Evidence that the Nazgûl are actually very sensitive creatures.
- **E.** A plan to lure the hobbits out of their hiding place.

### Question 5 `[CIM/ANT]` `[Advanced]`

Sam’s reaction to the "white star" twinkling among the cloud-wrack—feeling that "the Shadow was only a small and passing thing"—marks a moment of:

> **Relevant Passage:**

- **A.** A visual hallucination caused by the "bitter tang" in the air.

- **C.** Evidence that Sam is becoming a professional astronomer.

### Question 6 `[PIS/SCA]` `[Advanced]`

The description of the Tower of Cirith Ungol as a place built "not to keep enemies out of Mordor, but to keep them in" highlights:

> **Relevant Passage:**

- **A.** The inefficiency of Gondorian military architecture.

- **C.** Evidence that the Orcs are afraid of the outside world.

### Question 7 `[CIM/PIS]` `[Advanced]`

Frodo’s insistence that "I'd rather have Gollum than orcs" because "he won't give us away to them" suggests:

> **Relevant Passage:**
> "'...I'd rather have Gollum than orcs. At any rate he won't give us away to them - not unless he's caught himself.'"

- **A.** Frodo’s total trust in Gollum’s friendship.
- **B.** A realistic assessment of Gollum’s absolute, singular obsession with the Ring, which makes him a reliable ally only in the sense that his greed for the "Precious" is fundamentally incompatible with Sauron’s possession of it.
- **C.** That Frodo is becoming as "mad" as Gollum.
- **D.** Evidence that Gollum is actually a secret agent for Gandalf.
- **E.** A sign that Frodo has forgotten Gollum’s attack at the tunnel.

### Question 8 `[ACN/SCA]` `[Advanced]`

> **Relevant Passage:**

- **C.** Evidence that the "Large Warrior" (Sam) is influencing the Orcs' behavior.
- **D.** A sign that the Orcs are starting to join the West.
- **E.** A humorous interlude to lighten the mood of the march.

### Question 10 `[ACN/ANT]` `[Advanced]`

The image of the hobbits' escape from the road—"crawling away out of the turmoil" until they "dropped over the further edge"—symbolizes:

> **Relevant Passage:**
> "Slowly on hand and knee the hobbits crawled away out of the turmoil... until at last unnoticed they dropped over the further edge of the road."

- **A.** Their failure to participate in the "great" battle at the Gate.
- **B.** The "un-Great" but essential triumph of the small and the unnoticed, where their very insignificance and "crawling" nature allow them to bypass the "turmoil" of the Great Powers and reach their final destination.
- **C.** Evidence that the hobbits are actually very bad at walking.
- **D.** A sign that the road was actually too high for them.
- **E.** A humorous ending to a long and difficult chapter.
