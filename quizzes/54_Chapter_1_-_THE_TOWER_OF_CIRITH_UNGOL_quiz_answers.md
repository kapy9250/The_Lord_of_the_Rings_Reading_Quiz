### Question 1 `[CIM/ANT]` `[Advanced]`

**Sam’s realization that "time was desperately precious" but that "perishing is more likely" reflects a character state of:**

**Correct Answer: B. A "High" heroic realism that accepts the near-certainty of death while remaining absolutely committed to a moral duty, choosing the "hard" path of action over the "easy" path of surrender.**

**Why this is correct:**
Sam’s grim assessment ("perishing is more likely, and will be a lot easier anyway") illustrates his transition into a higher-order hero. He is not motivated by a "hope" of survival, but by a "duty" to his master. His choice to walk back through the tunnel alone, despite his "quaking knees," is a supreme act of will that prioritizes moral integrity over biological survival.

**Why E is incorrect** `[OI]`:
On the contrary, his choice to "rescue his master or perish" is the ultimate expression of his duty—a "High" Shire-honour that has been refined by his journey into the Land of Shadow.

### Question 2 `[ACN/ANT]` `[Advanced]`

**The description of the "sullen glow of red" beneath the "vast vapours" of Mordor, and the tower windows looking like "small red eyes," serves to:**

**Correct Answer: B. Physically and symbolically manifest the "all-seeing" and malevolent nature of the land, where the environment itself becomes a predatory extension of the Dark Lord’s surveillance.**

**Why this is correct:**
The narrator describes the light as "sullen" and the windows as "eyes." This personification of the landscape emphasizes that in Mordor, there is no "neutral" ground. The very atmosphere is imbued with the Eye's "evil vigilance." It creates a sensory environment of constant, malicious observation, heightening the psychological pressure on the "unseen" hobbit.

**Why A is incorrect** `[PT]`:
While Mount Doom *is* active, the specific imagery of the "red eyes" in the *tower windows* focuses on the theme of surveillance and the presence of the Orcs/Sauron, rather than a purely geological prediction.

### Question 3 `[CIM/PIS]` `[Advanced]`

**Sam’s vision of himself as "Samwise the Strong, Hero of the Age," turning Gorgoroth into a garden, reveals that the Ring’s temptation exploits:**

**Correct Answer: B. His most noble and fundamental identity (the gardener), twisting his altruistic desire for beauty and "order" into a tool for absolute, individual "Command."**

**Why this is correct:**
The Ring doesn't tempt Sam with gold or jewelry; it tempts him with a "garden of flowers and trees" across all of Gorgoroth. This shows how the Ring exploits the "Good" in a person to lead them toward "Mastery." By offering a "perfect" version of his own dream, it tries to convince Sam that absolute power is necessary to achieve his humble goals.

**Why A is incorrect** `[SL]`:
Sam identifies the vision as a "cheat" and a "trick." The text emphasizes that "Hero of the Age" is a label the *Ring* offers him, rather than a pre-existing ambition of his own.

### Question 4 `[CIM/SCA]` `[Advanced]`

**What does Sam’s "plain hobbit-sense"—knowing he was "not large enough to bear such a burden"—suggest about the nature of resistance to the Ring?**

**Correct Answer: B. That a grounded, humble self-knowledge and the rejection of "Mastery" over others are the most effective psychological defenses against the Ring's promise of power.**

**Why this is correct:**
Sam’s "plain hobbit-sense" is his salvation. He recognizes that he only needs "one small garden," not a realm. His rejection of the "High" vision is based on a realistic assessment of his own nature. This humility acts as a spiritual "refusal" that the Ring cannot overcome, proving that the "small" are less vulnerable to the Ring's logic of grandiosity.

**Why E is incorrect** `[SL]`:
The text presents his self-assessment as a form of *strength* and "plain sense," rather than a negative self-image or a belief in his own "unimportance." His "littleness" is his moral shield.

### Question 5 `[PIS/SCA]` `[Advanced]`

**The "Two Watchers" at the gate, described as "aware" but "immovable," represent a form of:**

**Correct Answer: B. A high-order, spiritual surveillance that bypasses physical concealment, manifesting the "un-sleeping" and collective malice of the tower’s "will."**

**Why this is correct:**
The Watchers are "aware" and "forbid his entry." They are not just statues; they possess a "spirit of evil vigilance." This represents the boundary between the "Seen" and "Unseen" worlds. Their power is not physical but "willful," creating a barrier that only a "Higher" spiritual force (the Phial) can break.

**Why C is incorrect** `[PT]`:
The narrator calls them "hideous" and emphasizes the "malice" in their eyes, characterizing them as supernatural guardians rather than purely artistic achievements.

### Question 6 `[ACN/ANT]` `[Advanced]`

**The image of the courtyard "strewn with bodies" grappling in "pairs, still grappling... in the very throes of stabbing, throttling, biting" emphasizes:**

**Correct Answer: B. The total, self-destructive "rot" of the Enemy's forces, where their inherent hatred and "bitter" rivalries result in a mutual annihilation that perfectly serves the "good" without intent.**

**Why this is correct:**
The Orcs have killed each other over "the swag" (Frodo's coat). This "swarming" self-destruction is a literalization of the proverb "oft evil will shall evil mar." Sauron's forces are undone by their own "black blood" and lack of fellowship, clearing the way for Sam in a way that "no other counsel" could have devised.

**Why E is incorrect** `[SL]`:
The tone is one of "stench," "death," and "hideous" violence. The description is intended to evoke the grim and self-consuming nature of evil, rather than humor.

### Question 7 `[CIM/ANT]` `[Advanced]`

**Sam’s decision to sing "old childish tunes out of the Shire" when he feels "finally defeated" serves to:**

**Correct Answer: B. Re-connect with his primary source of spiritual strength (the Shire), using the "small" beauty of his home to displace the "total" darkness of the tower and ignite a final, unbidden burst of hope.**

**Why this is correct:**
Sam’s song is an act of defiance against the "dead end." He starts with "childish tunes" and moves to his own words about the Sun and Stars. This vocalizes his core belief: "I will not say the Day is done." By singing of the "western lands" in the heart of Mordor, Sam uses his cultural identity to reclaim his "self" from the despair of the tower.

**Why E is incorrect** `[OI]`:
Sam calls his own voice "thin and quavering," distinguishing it from the "clear song of an Elven-lord." His victory is one of *spirit* and *memory*, not musical talent.

### Question 8 `[CIM/PIS]` `[Advanced]`

**Frodo’s immediate reaction to Sam having the Ring—calling him a "thief" and seeing him as a "foul little creature"—reveals:**

**Correct Answer: B. The terrifying, addictive "Mastery" of the Ring, which distorts the bearer’s perception of reality to protect itself, turning the ultimate act of loyalty into an appearance of betrayal.**

**Why this is correct:**
The "hideous vision" was "so real" to Frodo. This shows how the Ring has "overthrown" his mind. It creates a hallucinatory state where his best friend appears as an Orc. This illustrates the Ring's primary defense: it isolates the bearer by making them unable to trust anyone, effectively "wraithing" their social and emotional perceptions.

**Why A is incorrect** `[PT]`:
The text explicitly states that "a mist seemed to clear from his eyes" after the outburst, and he cries "O Sam! What have I said? ... It is the horrible power of the Ring." His reaction is an externalized symptom of the Ring's influence, not a reflection of his true character.

### Question 9 `[PIS/SCA]` `[Advanced]`

**Frodo’s explanation that the Shadow "can only mock, it cannot make" provides a philosophical framework for understanding:**

**Correct Answer: B. The derivative and parasitic nature of evil, which relies on the corruption and perversion of existing life because it lacks the creative, divine "Light" needed to produce anything original.**

**Why this is correct:**
Frodo explains that the Shadow "only ruined... and twisted" the Orcs. This is a central point of Tolkien's theology: evil is not a creative force but a "black shadow" that exists only by distorting the "Good." The Orcs must eat and drink because they are still biological beings, despite their spiritual corruption. Their existence is a "mockery" of the original design.

**Why E is incorrect** `[SL]`:
While Orcs were once related to other races (Elves, in lore), Frodo's point is about the *nature of evil’s creative power* (or lack thereof) rather than a simple aesthetic judgment of their appearance.

### Question 10 `[CIM/SCA]` `[Advanced]`

**Sam’s use of the Phial at the gate, crying "_Gilthoniel, A Elbereth!_", represents a "Higher" victory because:**

**Correct Answer: B. It demonstrates that the simple, "unthinking" invocation of the West’s spiritual guardians is a more effective weapon against the "malice" of the Watchers than any physical force or Ring-power.**

**Why this is correct:**
Sam’s thought "sprang back suddenly to the Elves in the Shire." His use of the "password" is an act of spiritual alignment. The Watchers’ will is broken not by "iron" but by the "dazzling radiance" of the West. It confirms that the "unimportant" hobbit, through his simple faith and memory, can wield a power that Mordor’s "sorcery" cannot withstand.

**Why E is incorrect** `[OI]`:
While the Nazgûl *does* respond to the "wail" of the Watchers, Sam's intent was to *break* the Watchers' will to escape, rather than to summon an enemy.
