### Question 1 `[CIM/ANT]` `[Advanced]`

**Frodo’s realization that "to go back is to admit defeat" and his subsequent sigh indicate:**

**Correct Answer: B. A heavy, somber acceptance of a duty that he finds personally undesirable but morally and strategically unavoidable.**

**Why this is correct:**
Frodo admits he "wishes" he was back in Rivendell, but his question about "shame" and "defeat" shows he is weighing his personal comfort against his ethical obligation. The "sigh" is the physical expression of this resignation—he chooses the "evil" road of Moria not because he wants adventure, but because he understands the catastrophic cost of failure.

**Analysis of Distractors:**
- **A** is a "Moralistic Misreading" that frames his resignation as "heroic enthusiasm."
- **C** is an "Over-Interpretation" that suspects Gandalf of "leading them into a trap."
- **D** is a "Half-Truth" about "shame" that misses the *strategic* weight of the decision.
- **E** is an "Academic Over-Interpretation" about "losing emotional connection."

### Question 2 `[PIS/SCA]` `[Advanced]`

**Gandalf’s argument for entering Moria ("the road at any rate that the Enemy will least expect us to take") reveals a strategy of:**

**Correct Answer: B. Psychological warfare based on counter-intuitive movement and the exploitation of the Enemy's own assumptions about risk.**

**Why this is correct:**
Gandalf's plan relies on the "unforeseen." He knows Sauron expects them to take the Road or the Gap of Rohan. By choosing a path that is "black" and feared even by the Wise (Moria), Gandalf is using the Enemy’s own logic of "power" against him, assuming Sauron won't expect his opponents to take such a "desperate" risk.

**Analysis of Distractors:**
- **A** is a "Surface-Level Literalism" about "direct military confrontation."
- **C** is a "Plausible Misreading" that frames the choice as purely "desperate" rather than "strategic."
- **D** is an "Over-Interpretation" about "lost records of the Dwarves."
- **E** is a "Lore-Trap" about the "Shadow being less effective underground."

### Question 3 `[CIM/ANT]` `[Advanced]`

**Aragorn’s warning to Gandalf—"if you pass the doors of Moria, beware!"—carries a subtext of:**

**Correct Answer: B. A prophetic or highly intuitive foreboding specifically concerning Gandalf’s personal safety and his ultimate sacrifice in the Mines.**

**Why this is correct:**
Aragorn explicitly states he is thinking "of you, Gandalf." As a Ranger with deep "intuition" and some degree of foresight (royal heritage), his warning serves as a narrative "ominous note" that points toward the specific danger Gandalf faces, rather than a general fear.

**Analysis of Distractors:**
- **A** is a "Moralistic Misreading" that suspects "resentment."
- **C** is a "Plausible Misreading" about "failing memory."
- **D** is a "Lore-Trap" about "Gandalf’s desire for the Ring."
- **E** is an "Over-Interpretation" about "seeing Gandalf’s death" previously.

### Question 4 `[ACN/ANT]` `[Advanced]`

**The "eye-opener" of Gandalf’s battle with the Wargs serves to:**

**Correct Answer: A. Contrast Gandalf’s "jovial" Shire persona with his true, terrifying nature as an angelic messenger and a wielder of elemental power.**

**Why this is correct:**
The hobbits usually see Gandalf's "jokes and toys." This scene, where he becomes a "great menacing shape like the monument of some ancient king," reveals his true identity as an Istari. It prepares the reader for the higher-stakes magic and the mythic scale of the conflict in Moria.

**Analysis of Distractors:**
- **B** is a "Lore-Trap" that attributes his power to the "Ring."
- **C** is an "Academic Over-Interpretation" using the phrase "elements that obey."
- **D** is a "Plausible Misreading" about the "King of stone in the Barrow-downs."
- **E** is a "Surface-Level Literalism" about "physical size."

### Question 5 `[CIM/ANT]` `[Advanced]`

### Question 6 `[PIS/SCA]` `[Advanced]`

**Gandalf’s struggle to find the opening word for the Doors of Durin, only to find it "absurdly simple," illustrates:**

**Correct Answer: A. A tragic irony where ancient, open-hearted "friendship" has become so alien to the modern "suspicious" mind that its simplest expressions are mistaken for complex secrets.**

**Why this is correct:**
Gandalf remarks that the word was "too simple for a learned lore-master in these suspicious days." The Door reflects a time when Elves and Dwarves were "friends." Gandalf's failure to see the answer highlights how the "Shadow" has corrupted even the Wise's ability to recognize simple goodness.

**Analysis of Distractors:**
- **B** is a "Half-Truth" about "learned status" that misses the broader *historical* tragedy.
- **C** is a "Comedy" distractor about a "public park."
- **D** is an "Over-Interpretation" about the Elvish language becoming "thinner."
- **E** is a "Surface-Level Literalism" about Gandalf's "preference" for complex spells.

Gandalf "did not speak aloud his thought" that the creature had "seized on Frodo first." This implies a supernatural resonance. The Watchman is a manifestation of the "older and fouler things" that are drawn to the Ring's presence.

- **D** is a "Half-Truth" about "tactical decisions" that misses the *supernatural* link.
- **E** is a "Plausible Misreading" about "lack of alertness."

### Question 10 `[CIM/ANT]` `[Advanced]`

**Frodo’s reaction to learning the value of his mithril-coat ("the price of the Shire under his jacket") primarily conveys:**

**Correct Answer: B. A profound feeling of "out-of-place" responsibility, where his physical person now carries the symbolic weight of his entire homeland’s value.**

**Why this is correct:**
The "price of the Shire" metaphor makes the burden literal. Frodo is "staggered" by the realization. This reinforces the theme of "Small vs. Great"—the "plain hobbit" is enveloped in a treasure of "kingly" proportions, highlighting the gravity of his role.

**Analysis of Distractors:**
- **A** is a "Surface-Level Literalism" that suspects "greed."
- **C** is a "Moralistic Misreading" about "resentment toward Bilbo."
- **D** is a "Plausible Misreading" about "fear of theft."
- **E** is an "Over-Interpretation" about becoming a "mithril-lord."
